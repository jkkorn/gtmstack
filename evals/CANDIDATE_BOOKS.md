# Candidate next books

The 5 books currently encoded as evals are *one cut* through the GTM
canon. They split along the funnel:

| Axis | Book |
|------|------|
| Where to play | Crossing the Chasm |
| What category | Play Bigger |
| How to position | Obviously Awesome |
| Whether demand is real | The Mom Test |
| How to sell | Predictable Revenue |

The next book gets picked by readers, not by me. This file is a seed
list — candidates I'd be glad to encode if voted in. Add your own as
a nomination (see "How to nominate" below); I'll add it to this list
when it lands.

> Order is *not* a ranking. It's grouped by which axis the book would
> reinforce or open up.

---

## Sales execution (currently thin)

**SPIN Selling** — Neil Rackham
The discipline behind enterprise discovery calls: Situation, Problem,
Implication, Need-payoff. Where Predictable Revenue covers role design
and pipeline construction, SPIN covers what happens inside a single call.
*What it would add: a deeper lens on complex-sale discovery, especially
relevant for high-ACV B2B and regulated-buyer cycles.*

**The Challenger Sale** — Matt Dixon, Brent Adamson
Teach-Tailor-Take-Control. The "challenger" rep outsells the
"relationship-builder" in complex, multi-stakeholder enterprise deals.
Worth pairing with Brazilian-GTM context module — Brazilian enterprise
sales is famously relationship-first, so Challenger findings would
either confirm or productively contradict.
*What it would add: closing-side discipline for multi-buyer enterprise
deals, and a sharp test against the "relationship sells" default.*

**From Impossible to Inevitable** — Aaron Ross, Jason Lemkin
Sequel to Predictable Revenue, focused on hypergrowth (10x-100x).
Nail-a-niche, create-predictable-pipeline, make-sales-scalable,
double-your-deal-size, do-the-time, embrace-employee-ownership,
define-your-destiny. Heavier on retention and expansion than the original.
*What it would add: expansion / land-and-expand / customer-success lens
the current set lacks.*

## Positioning / messaging (foundational gap)

**Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind** — Al Ries, Jack Trout
The book Dunford's Obviously Awesome was built on top of. The "be first
in a category" thesis, the over-communicated society argument, the
mental-position-not-product-position distinction. Older than the other
books on this list (1981) but still load-bearing.
*What it would add: the underlying theory of positioning that makes
Dunford make sense, plus a historical lens on what changes vs. what
doesn't across decades.*

**Building a StoryBrand** — Donald Miller
Customer-as-hero narrative framework. Seven-part story structure
applied to website copy and sales messaging. More tactical and
copy-focused than the strategic books.
*What it would add: a copy-craft lens, useful for the website / landing
page / email beat that gtmstack currently doesn't touch.*

## Channel discovery / early growth

**Traction** — Gabriel Weinberg, Justin Mares
19 marketing channels framework + the Bullseye method (test three
channels per quarter, double down on the one that pulls). The
operating-manual book for early-stage founders deciding which growth
channels to invest in.
*What it would add: a structured channel-portfolio lens — currently
gtmstack assumes the team has picked a motion; Traction would diagnose
the picking itself.*

**Hacking Growth** — Sean Ellis, Morgan Brown
The "growth team" operating model — North Star Metric, growth hacking
process, retention loops, viral loops. Heavier on quantitative
experimentation than Traction.
*What it would add: experimentation discipline + retention-loop framing.
Most relevant for consumer / PLG.*

## Strategy / category creation

**Zero to One** — Peter Thiel
Monopoly-or-bust thesis, contrarian truths, secrets, last-mover
advantage. Closer to a philosophy than a framework, but the
monopoly-thinking lens cuts through "we have lots of competitors but
that's fine" delusions.
*What it would add: a sharper monopoly / power-law lens. Productive
tension with Play Bigger (both about winning a category, different
emphasis on how).*

**Blue Ocean Strategy** — W. Chan Kim, Renée Mauborgne
Value innovation through the four-actions framework (eliminate / reduce
/ raise / create) and the strategy canvas. Classic "find the
uncontested market space" book.
*What it would add: a visual / canvas-based lens on category strategy.
Complementary to Play Bigger's category-design angle.*

**The Innovator's Dilemma** — Clayton Christensen
Disruption theory — incumbents fail not because they're stupid but
because the rational decisions of well-run companies blind them to
low-end and new-market entrants. Plus Jobs-to-be-Done framing in
later work.
*What it would add: a structural lens on incumbent vulnerability,
especially relevant for AI-native entrants targeting category leaders.*

## Customer development / discovery (adjacent to Mom Test)

**The Four Steps to the Epiphany** — Steve Blank
The founding text of customer development (predates Lean Startup).
Customer Discovery → Customer Validation → Customer Creation →
Company Building. More formal and process-heavy than Mom Test.
*What it would add: an end-to-end customer-development process. Mom
Test is a tactic; Four Steps is the strategy.*

**Demand-Side Sales 101** — Bob Moesta
Jobs-to-be-Done applied directly to selling. Pull-vs-push, the four
forces of progress (push of the situation, pull of the new solution,
anxiety of the new, habit of the present). Switch-interview methodology.
*What it would add: JTBD as a sales / qualification lens. Different
from Mom Test's customer-discovery angle.*

## How to nominate

Open an issue using the **"Nominate a book"** template:
[New nomination →](https://github.com/jonathan-korn/gtmstack/issues/new?template=book-nomination.yml)

Or DM me. I tally regularly and add the winner as the next encoded book.

If your book isn't on this list but should be, nominate it anyway and
say why. The list is a starting point, not a ballot.

## How a book gets picked

I look for three things, in this order:

1. **Cumulative vote count + comment depth.** Someone leaving a
   one-line "+1 SPIN" carries less weight than someone leaving 200
   words on *why* SPIN would change their work.
2. **Coverage of an underweight axis.** If the current 5 books all
   pile up on positioning and none cover sales execution, the next
   book should rebalance.
3. **Whether the book has a *codifiable* framework.** Books that are
   60% narrative and 40% wisdom-quotes are harder to encode as a
   rubric than books with a named, structured methodology. Both kinds
   work, but the codifiable ones are faster to ship.

The vote is real, not theater. If a book I don't love wins by a wide
margin, it gets encoded. The point is to be useful to operators, not
to flatter my taste.
