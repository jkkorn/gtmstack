# Framework: Obviously Awesome (April Dunford)

> Source: Dunford, April. *Obviously Awesome.* Ambient Press, 2019. This file
> summarizes the analytical lens in our own words for the purpose of building
> runnable evals. It does not reproduce Dunford's prose. Cite the book.

## The lens, in one paragraph

Dunford's claim is that positioning is not branding, messaging, or taglines.
Positioning is the deliberate choice of a *frame of reference* — a market
category — that makes a product's strengths obvious to the customer who needs
them most. Most products are positioned by *default* (whatever the founder
first called it) rather than by *deliberate choice*, and the result is products
that look like inferior versions of established categories instead of category-
defining options. The job of positioning is to pick the frame in which the
product wins, then give it the language and proof to register that win in the
customer's head before the customer evaluates alternatives.

## The five components of positioning

| Component | What it answers | Failure mode |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Competitive alternatives | What would the customer do without you? | Naming "the market" or generic competitors instead of specific named alternatives, including non-obvious ones (legacy tools, manual processes, "do nothing"). |
| Unique attributes | What features/capabilities do you have that the alternatives lack? | Listing features that competitors also have, or naming generic attributes ("better UX") instead of specific capabilities. |
| Value themes | What outcomes do those attributes enable for customers? | Generic outcomes ("more productive") instead of specific time/money/risk/capability changes. |
| Best-fit customers | Who values those attributes most? | Category-level segments ("SMBs," "enterprises") instead of role-level + named industry/size. |
| Market category | What frame of reference triggers the right associations? | Defaulting to the obvious category and competing as an inferior option, instead of picking a frame in which the unique attributes matter. |

## The asymmetry principle

Strong positioning makes the asymmetry of value visible: who *loves* this product
should be paired with who *should not buy* this product. Sharp positioning is
exclusionary by design — naming the worst-fit customer is part of the work, not
an oversight. A positioning that doesn't repel anyone isn't positioning, it's
description.

## The category choice

The most consequential positioning decision is the market category. The default
choice is whatever the product is most obviously *like* (a CRM is "a CRM," a
project tool is "a project tool"). This default usually positions the product
as an inferior version of an established category. The deliberate choice picks
a frame where the unique attributes are central, not peripheral.

Three category strategies (in increasing risk and reward):
1. **Compete in an existing category** — fastest customer comprehension,
   highest direct competitive pressure, easy to be "yet another."
2. **Pick a niche subcategory inside an existing category** — narrows the
   audience but sharpens the differentiation, slower comprehension but lower
   direct competition.
3. **Define a new category** — highest leverage if it lands; requires investment
   to educate the market on the new frame; not every product can or should.

## The trend layer

Trends (AI, climate, geopolitics, regulatory shifts, generational behavior)
can amplify positioning when they make the unique attributes more relevant.
Layering a trend onto positioning that doesn't actually benefit from it produces
noise — every product claims to be "AI-powered" today and the claim has lost
discriminating power. The discipline: invoke a trend only when it concretely
strengthens the case, and explicitly state why; otherwise decline with rationale.

## What disqualifies a positioning candidate

- Best-fit customer is too broad ("knowledge workers," "growing companies").
- Market category is the default obvious one and competitor advantages dominate
  the frame.
- Value themes are restatements of attributes (not outcomes) or generic benefits.
- No worst-fit customer named — the positioning doesn't repel anyone.
- The positioning statement could equally describe two competitors.

## Common positioning failure modes (what this eval should catch)

- **Default category trap.** Positioning the product in the most obvious
  category, where established competitors dominate the frame. The product
  becomes "an inferior X."
- **Attribute soup.** Listing features without organizing them into value
  themes, leaving the customer to figure out why the attributes matter.
- **Generic best-fit.** Best-fit customer is a category, not a role within a
  named segment.
- **No worst-fit.** Positioning is described in a way that "everyone could be
  the customer." Universally desired = universally undifferentiated.
- **Trend slop.** Layering "AI" or other current trends without explaining why
  the trend strengthens this specific positioning. Indistinguishable from
  every other product making the same claim.
- **Positioning by tagline.** Treating positioning as a sentence-craft exercise
  rather than a category/frame choice — produces witty taglines that don't
  change which competitors customers compare you to.

## What "good Obviously Awesome analysis" looks like (this is what we score)

A good analysis applied to a real GTM situation should:
1. Name competitive alternatives specifically, including non-obvious ones (status
   quo, manual workflow, doing nothing).
2. Identify at least 3 unique attributes that ALL the named competitive
   alternatives lack — concrete capabilities, not generic strengths.
3. Map each attribute to a value theme that connects it to a specific customer
   outcome (time saved, revenue generated, risk reduced, capability unlocked).
4. Specify the best-fit customer at role-level within a named industry/size band.
5. Pick a market category — not the default obvious one — and justify it
   explicitly. Name what choosing that category gains and what it gives up.
6. Name the worst-fit customer — the asymmetry principle in action.
7. Either layer a relevant trend with a specific rationale OR decline to layer a
   trend with rationale. No silent trend-claiming.
8. Produce a tight positioning statement (1-2 sentences) that combines best-fit
   customer + value theme + competitive alternative.

## What the eval should *not* score

- Whether the analysis quotes the book.
- Whether the analysis uses Dunford's exact terminology.
- Whether the analyst's category choice matches some "right answer" — there
  often isn't one. What's scored is whether the choice is justified and the
  alternatives considered.
