# Framework: Play Bigger (Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead, Kevin Maney)

> Source: Ramadan, Al; Peterson, Dave; Lochhead, Christopher; Maney, Kevin.
> *Play Bigger: How Pirates, Dreamers, and Innovators Create and Dominate
> Markets.* HarperBusiness, 2016. This file summarizes the analytical lens
> in our own words for the purpose of building runnable evals. It does not
> reproduce the authors' prose. Cite the book.

## The lens, in one paragraph

Play Bigger's claim is that the companies that win at scale are those that
design and dominate new market categories — not those that build "better"
versions of existing categories. Their data point: category kings (the
single dominant company in a category) capture roughly 76% of the total
market capitalization created in the category's lifetime. Everyone else
splits the remaining ~24%. The implication: being #2 or #3 in an existing
category is structurally a worse outcome than being #1 in a new category
you defined. *Category design* — the work of identifying a mission-critical
problem, crystallizing a Point of View on why a new category must exist,
firing a coordinated lightning strike to frame the category in the market,
and aligning Product, Company, and Category internally — is the highest-
order strategic move available to a startup or expansion. Most teams skip
it, default to the obvious existing category, and concede category-king
economics from day one.

## The Magic Triangle

Three disciplines that must align for category king status:

| Discipline | What it answers |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Product** | What you build, the technical and experiential capabilities |
| **Company** | How you organize, fund, scale, who you hire, what you value |
| **Category** | The new market frame you're claiming, the problem named |

When all three align, category-king status is achievable. Misalignment
shows up as: a great product in a category that doesn't exist yet (no
demand frame), a strong company organized for the wrong category (sales
motion vs. product reality mismatch), or a category claim a product can't
deliver on (vapor positioning). The job is to design all three together.

## Point of View (POV)

The clearest statement of why this category should exist. A real POV
names three things:

1. **The mission-critical problem people have today** — usually unnamed or
   poorly named before the category exists. ("Inbound marketing" was the
   category HubSpot named for the problem of "marketing interrupts and
   feels broken.")
2. **Why existing solutions don't and cannot solve it** — not "ours is
   better"; "theirs is structurally wrong because they were built for a
   different problem."
3. **The new way of thinking the category requires** — the conceptual
   shift the buyer must make to recognize the new category as the answer.

A sharp POV is uncomfortable. It excludes. It names winners and losers in
the marketplace of ideas before the market itself has chosen.

## Lightning strike

The coordinated launch event that frames the category in the market. Not
just a product launch — a category launch. Lightning strikes succeed when:

- The product is ready to deliver on the category claim
- The company is funded and staffed to defend the category over years
- The narrative infrastructure (content, customer stories, analyst
  briefings, conference presence, partnerships, language) is in place to
  reinforce the POV consistently

Premature lightning strike (firing before infrastructure exists) burns the
category claim — competitors and analysts learn to ignore the company.
Late lightning strike (after a competitor has framed the category)
concedes category king status. Timing is load-bearing.

## Category sequencing

1. **Discover and design** — identify the mission-critical problem.
   Crystallize the POV. Name what existing categories miss.
2. **Frame the category** — lightning strike. Establish language. Control
   drift.
3. **Build the company that wins** — Product / Company / Category aligned.
   Hire to the category, not to existing roles.
4. **Govern the category** — once won, defend the POV. Don't let
   competitors redefine the boundaries. Keep adding to the category from
   inside.

## When NOT to design a new category

Category design is high-risk, high-reward. It's wrong when:

- The problem is real but a *good-enough* category already exists with
  room for a strong #2 (sometimes #2 in a big existing category beats #1
  in a small new one)
- The team / funding / patience for category education don't exist
- The product can't deliver on a defensible category claim (vapor
  positioning destroys credibility long-term)
- An existing category is itself in a category-king transition (a new #1
  is being decided right now in an existing category — winning that race
  is more valuable than starting a new one)
- The expansion is geographic / segment-narrowing rather than truly
  category-creating (entering a new country with the same product is
  usually market entry, not category design)

A correct Play Bigger analysis on a company in any of these situations
should *recommend competing in an existing category strategically* and
explicitly explain why category design is wrong for now.

## Common category-design failure modes (what this eval should catch)

- **Default category trap.** Falling into the obvious existing category
  by inertia, conceding category-king status to whoever was first.
- **Better thinking, not different thinking.** Positioning as "10x faster
  X" or "cheaper Y" — these are improvements within an existing category,
  not a new category claim.
- **Vapor POV.** A category claim the product cannot deliver on, or a POV
  so generic it could equally describe three competitors.
- **Premature lightning strike.** Firing the category-launch sequence
  before the product, company, and narrative infrastructure are ready.
- **Magic triangle misalignment.** Product is built for one category, the
  Company is organized for another, the Category claim is a third. The
  result is a confused buyer.
- **Marketing drift.** The POV is sharp at launch and slowly dilutes as
  marketing teams chase quarterly metrics. The category gets recaptured
  by a competitor with cleaner language.
- **#2 by default.** Conceding category king status because the existing
  category leader is "too established" — when in reality the category
  itself is ripe for redefinition.

## What "good Play Bigger analysis" looks like (this is what we score)

A good analysis applied to a real GTM situation should:

1. Name the mission-critical problem, distinguishing it from the existing
   "obvious" problem framing in the market today.
2. State a clear Point of View (POV) on the category, with all three
   parts (problem, why existing solutions fail, new way of thinking).
3. Diagnose the Magic Triangle alignment — Product / Company / Category
   internally consistent or visibly misaligned.
4. Recommend a category strategy: design a new category (with named
   category and stated POV), claim a niche subcategory inside an existing
   one, or compete for category king status in an existing category. Each
   path is valid; the recommendation must be reasoned.
5. Sequence the lightning strike (or explicitly defer it) — what
   infrastructure must exist first, what trigger event launches the
   category claim publicly.
6. Flag failure modes with cited evidence — default category trap, better
   thinking, vapor POV, premature lightning strike, magic triangle
   misalignment, marketing drift.
7. Confidence calibration on the most contested calls (POV sharpness,
   category strategy choice).

## What the eval should *not* score

- Whether the analysis quotes the book.
- Whether the analysis recommends category design over competing-in-
  existing. Either is valid if reasoned.
- Whether the analyst agrees with Play Bigger's contested claim that 76%
  of category value goes to the king. Critical engagement is fine.
