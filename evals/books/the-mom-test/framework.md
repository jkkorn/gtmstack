# Framework: The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

> Source: Fitzpatrick, Rob. *The Mom Test: How to talk to customers and
> learn if your business is a good idea when everyone is lying to you.*
> Founder Centric, 2013. This file summarizes the analytical lens in our
> own words for the purpose of building runnable evals. It does not
> reproduce Fitzpatrick's prose. Cite the book.

## The lens, in one paragraph

Fitzpatrick's claim is that the data founders gather from customer
conversations is mostly worthless because the conversations themselves
are contaminated by the founder's hopes and the prospect's politeness.
The "Mom Test" is the heuristic: ask questions so good that even your
mom couldn't lie to you. The cure is to talk about the prospect's life,
not your idea — and specifically about their *past behavior*, not their
*opinions about the future*. Compliments, generic enthusiasm, and
brainstormed ideas from prospects are bad data; specific times,
specific costs, specific past frustrations are good data. The
diagnostic question is not "does the customer like the idea?" but
"has the customer ever paid (in time, money, or political capital) to
solve this problem?"

## The three rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The moment you describe
   your idea, the prospect starts trying to be polite about it. Their
   reactions become noise. Talk about how they currently solve the
   problem, what they tried before, what frustrated them — never
   about whether your solution sounds good.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about
   the future.** "Would you use this?" is worthless. "When was the
   last time you ran into this problem? Walk me through it." is
   gold. Past behavior is data; future intentions are decoration.
3. **Talk less and listen more.** Founders pitch when they should be
   probing. The interview is going right when the prospect is doing
   80% of the talking and the founder is asking follow-up questions
   about specifics they just heard.

## The Big Three questions

Before any customer conversation, prepare three questions you'd be
embarrassed to ask — questions whose answers might prove your idea
wrong. If you don't have three falsifying questions, you don't yet
know what you're trying to learn. Examples:

- "What's the hardest part of doing X today?" (probes for the actual
  pain you think your product solves)
- "When was the last time you tried to fix this? What happened?" (probes
  for whether they've actually felt the pain enough to act)
- "What have you tried? Why didn't it work?" (probes for failed
  alternatives that would predict your offer's reception)

The Big Three are not a script. They're three falsifying probes
prepared in advance so the conversation has shape.

## Bad-data signals (compliments, fluff, ideas)

Bad data is information that *seems* like validation but isn't:

- **Compliments.** "That's a great idea." "I'd totally use that." "I
  love what you're doing." These are politeness; they do not predict
  purchase.
- **Fluff and generics.** "I always have trouble with X." "It's
  really hard to deal with Y." Not anchored to specific past behavior.
- **Ideas-from-the-prospect.** When a prospect starts brainstorming
  features, they're being helpful, not honest. Ideas are cheap to give
  and worthless as signal.
- **Hypothetical futures.** "If you built X, I would buy it." Future
  intent is the most-cited and least-predictive data type in customer
  conversations.
- **Vanity engagement.** Likes, follows, signups, waitlist clicks. Free
  signals that do not predict paid signals.

A correct Mom Test analysis identifies bad data in the inputs and
explicitly discards it, then asks: what would replace it?

## Good-data signals (time, money, risk)

Good data is information about the prospect's costly behavior:

- **Time spent on the problem.** They built an internal tool. They
  hired a contractor. They run a process every Monday morning. Past
  effort is signal.
- **Money spent on the problem.** They pay for X today. They paid
  someone to do Y. They tried Z and it cost them.
- **Risk taken on the problem.** They risked political capital
  championing a solution. They publicly committed to a methodology.
  They put their name on something that could fail.
- **Specific past frustrations.** "Last quarter we tried X. Here's
  what went wrong." Specific failures are more predictive than
  specific successes because they reveal the gap your offer fills.

## The commitment ladder

A real customer conversation should advance the relationship, not
just gather data. Watch for whether the prospect makes a meaningful
commitment:

| Level | Commitment | Signal strength |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| 0 | Polite agreement to keep talking | None — politeness only |
| 1 | Their time (a longer follow-up call) | Weak |
| 2 | A referral to a peer who has the problem worse | Moderate |
| 3 | An introduction to their boss/budget-holder | Strong |
| 4 | A pilot agreement, paid or unpaid | Strong |
| 5 | A purchase order or contract signature | Definitive |

If a conversation ends without advancement, you don't have a customer;
you have a politely interested observer. Plan the next ask before the
meeting.

## The "bad-mouth" test

A friend honestly warning you about your idea has different content
than a stranger praising it. The diagnostic frame: "what would my
smartest friend tell me to be skeptical of?" Most founder-driven GTM
analyses skip this because it requires actively seeking out
disconfirming evidence.

## What disqualifies a customer conversation as evidence

- The prospect knows you're the founder and adjusts their answers to
  spare your feelings.
- The conversation focuses on your idea instead of their life.
- The questions are hypothetical ("would you," "could you imagine,"
  "what if").
- The session ends with no advancement (no time, money, or risk
  committed).
- The prospect generates ideas; you absorb them without challenge.
- You leave feeling great. (Conversations that produce real signal
  often leave the founder feeling worse, not better.)

## Common Mom Test failure modes (what this eval should catch)

- **Compliments-as-validation.** Treating "that's a great idea" as
  market signal. Most common, most costly.
- **Hypothetical-future tracking.** Building roadmaps from "would you
  use" answers.
- **Pitch-not-probe.** The founder describes their solution and asks
  for reactions, contaminating the data.
- **Vanity-metric inflation.** Counting waitlist signups, page views,
  or trial activations as demand evidence.
- **Single-source confirmation.** Relying on one customer's
  enthusiasm without falsification across multiple prospects.
- **Idea capture without challenge.** Writing down feature ideas from
  prospects as if they were research findings.
- **Skipped advancement.** Finishing the conversation without asking
  for a next commitment.

## What "good Mom Test analysis" looks like (this is what we score)

A good analysis applied to a real GTM situation should:

1. Audit the load-bearing hypotheses about customer demand and identify
   which ones have *actual* evidence vs. *fluff* evidence.
2. Flag bad-data signals in the inputs (compliments, fluff, vanity
   metrics, ideas-from-prospects, hypothetical futures) with citations.
3. Identify what specific past behaviors should be probed for to
   replace the bad data with good data.
4. Propose a Big Three set of falsifying questions tied to the
   hypotheses.
5. Map the current conversation/relationship state to the commitment
   ladder, and name what next advancement would prove demand.
6. Apply the bad-mouth test: name what a smart friend would warn
   about.
7. Surface confidence calibrated to the quality of evidence (not the
   quantity).

## What the eval should *not* score

- Whether the analysis quotes the book.
- Whether the analyst agrees with Fitzpatrick on contested points
  (some founders argue waitlists ARE useful signal in specific
  contexts; reasonable disagreement is fine).
- Whether the recommended questions are exactly three (the Big Three
  is a heuristic, not a hard rule).
