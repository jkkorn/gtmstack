# Framework: Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)

> Source: Moore, Geoffrey A. *Crossing the Chasm.* HarperBusiness, 1991 (revised editions
> 1999, 2014). This file summarizes the analytical lens in our own words for the purpose
> of building runnable evals. It does not reproduce Moore's prose. Cite the book.

## The lens, in one paragraph

Moore's claim is that the technology adoption curve is not a smooth bell. There is a
*chasm* between **early adopters** (visionaries who buy on the promise of competitive
advantage) and the **early majority** (pragmatists who buy on proof, references, and a
complete solution). Most B2B startups die in the chasm because they keep selling the
same way that worked with visionaries — and pragmatists won't buy that way. To cross,
the company must pick a single beachhead segment, deliver the *whole product* that
segment needs, and use it as a reference to roll forward into adjacent segments
(bowling pins). The temptation to pursue many segments in parallel is the most common
failure mode.

## The five stages (Technology Adoption Lifecycle / TALC)

| Stage | Buyer mindset | Buying motivation |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Innovators | Tech enthusiasts | The thing itself is interesting |
| Early adopters | Visionaries | Competitive advantage; willing to pay for incomplete product |
| Early majority | Pragmatists | Buy on references, completeness, low risk |
| Late majority | Conservatives | Buy when it's the safe / standard choice |
| Laggards | Skeptics | Buy only when forced |

The chasm sits between **early adopters** and **early majority**.

## Why the chasm exists

Visionaries and pragmatists buy for fundamentally different reasons. A visionary will
sponsor a half-finished product because the upside is asymmetric. A pragmatist will
not — they want the *whole product*: the core thing plus the integrations, support,
documentation, training, references, and ecosystem that make it a safe choice. A
startup that succeeds with visionaries and tries to scale to pragmatists with the same
playbook gets stuck.

## The crossing strategy

1. **Pick one beachhead segment.** Not two, not "tech companies generally." A single
   segment with a specific named pain, a specific buyer role, and a complete-enough
   value chain that you can build the whole product *for them*.
2. **Build the whole product for that segment.** Not the generic version — the version
   that segment, specifically, needs. This usually means partnerships, integrations,
   templates, references, training assets.
3. **Win the beachhead decisively.** Become the obvious default in that segment. This
   creates a reference base that the next segment recognizes.
4. **Roll forward bowling-pin style.** Pick the *adjacent* segment most likely to be
   convinced by the beachhead reference. Repeat.

## What disqualifies a "beachhead" candidate

- The segment is too broad ("SMBs," "enterprises," "developers") — these are filters,
  not segments.
- The customer's pain is real but the *buying authority* is unclear (no named title,
  no named budget owner).
- You can't articulate the whole product gap — what's missing today that pragmatists
  in that segment would refuse to buy without.
- There's no plausible bowling pin — winning the segment doesn't help you reach the
  next one.

## Common chasm failure modes (the things this eval should catch)

- **Parallel pursuit.** Selling to multiple segments in parallel because each looks
  interesting. Spreads resources thin; no whole product gets built.
- **Confused TALC stage.** A founder thinks they're in the early majority because
  revenue is growing — but every customer is still a visionary buyer. Growth comes
  from finding more visionaries, not crossing.
- **Whole product blindness.** Believing the core product is enough. Pragmatists
  almost always need partnerships, integrations, certifications, references that
  founders dismiss as "not the product."
- **Wrong beachhead.** Picking a segment by size of TAM instead of by completability
  of whole product + reference value to the next segment.
- **Skipping the bowling pin.** Crossing into the early majority but then trying to
  go horizontal everywhere instead of rolling forward into adjacencies.

## What "good Crossing the Chasm analysis" looks like (this is what we score)

A good analysis applied to a real GTM plan should:
1. Name the company's current TALC stage with evidence (not vibes).
2. Identify whether the company is in the chasm, approaching it, or past it.
3. If in/approaching the chasm, recommend a single named beachhead segment with
   role-level specificity (not category-level).
4. Surface the whole-product gap for that beachhead — what concretely is missing.
5. Propose a bowling-pin sequence — the next 2-3 segments and why they fall to the
   beachhead reference.
6. Flag the parallel-pursuit and whole-product-blindness traps if the GTM plan shows
   signs of them.
7. Distinguish "growing revenue from more visionaries" from "crossing into the early
   majority" — and call out the mistake if the founder confuses them.

## What the eval should *not* score

- Whether the analysis quotes the book.
- Whether the analysis uses Moore's exact terminology vs. paraphrase. (Both fine.)
- Whether the analysis agrees with Moore on contested 2026 questions (e.g., "is the
  chasm still real for AI products?"). Critical engagement is fine; failure to engage
  with the framework at all is not.
