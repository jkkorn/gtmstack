# Demand-Evidence Audit: gtmstack (self-diagnostic, 2026-05-03)

> Frame: gtmstack running The Mom Test on its own GTM. The author authored the
> books, the rubrics, the case file, and is now reading the audit. By Mom Test
> logic, every "signal" in the input is presumed contaminated until specific
> past behavior involving time, money, or risk is demonstrated. The job here is
> not to be encouraging. It is to separate signal from noise and to name what
> would replace the noise.

## Hypothesis Audit

The case is operating on five load-bearing hypotheses about customer demand. For
each, the input itself names the evidence — and, on inspection, almost all of
that evidence is fluff.

**H1. "GTM leads at Series B/C startups stuck on beachhead choice will value a
multi-book diagnostic in 25 minutes vs. $20K-$50K and 3 weeks of consulting."**
Evidence in inputs: a friend at a Series B AI startup said "I'd run this on our
GTM next week"; a friend at a YC-network startup said "this is the kind of
thing we'd pay for." **Classification: fluff.** Two friends, two
hypothetical futures ("I'd run," "we'd pay"), zero past-behavior events. The
input itself confirms: "No follow-through has occurred — no live-problem run,
no introduction to a peer, no pricing inquiry, no contract."

**H2. "gtmstack occupies a competitive slot no one else occupies — the only tool
that runs canonical GTM books as runnable rubrics with multi-book synthesis."**
Evidence in inputs: the author's own competitive map (Lavender/Outreach automate
execution; ChatGPT answers questions but doesn't encode rubrics; consulting is
slow). **Classification: fluff.** This is a founder-authored category map, not a
prospect's articulation of an unmet need. The input concedes: "AI-strategic-
thinking peers (uncertain — possibly nothing exists yet) would be the closest
comp. This positioning has not been tested with prospects." A category nobody has
asked for is not a category yet; it is a hypothesis.

**H3. "The multi-book synthesis is correct / useful — it prescribes moves real
operators would take, not just moves they would nod at."** Evidence in inputs: the
author has run gtmstack on cursor-2026 and anthropic-brazil-2026 and judges that
both syntheses "feel right" and "align with each company's observable strategic
moves." **Classification: fluff (and, structurally, the most dangerous one).**
"Feels right" is the founder grading their own homework. "Aligns with observable
strategic moves" is post-hoc fit to public information the author already knew
before running the eval. The author wrote the books, the rubrics, the cases, and
is judging the outputs. There is no out-of-sample test on a problem the author
did not pre-frame.

**H4. "gstack's audience overlaps with gtmstack's audience — the shape inherits the
audience."** Evidence in inputs: gstack has visible adoption in startup-founder
circles and gtmstack is patterned on it. **Classification: fluff.** The input
explicitly flags this: "The hypothesis that gstack's audience overlaps with
gtmstack's audience has not been validated." Shape-similarity is not
audience-similarity; gstack solves a builder's coding-skill-pack problem, gtmstack
proposes a strategy-diagnostic problem. Different jobs-to-be-done, possibly
non-overlapping buyers.

**H5. "The thing the founder makes is the thing operators want — the dogfooding
proves usefulness."** Evidence in inputs: "the author runs gtmstack on questions
that come up in advisory conversations and finds the synthesis useful for
structuring the author's own thinking." **Classification: fluff.** The input
itself names this honestly: "This is an n=1 use case where the founder is the user.
It is not customer evidence; it is dogfooding." Dogfooding proves the tool is
buildable and that one specific user finds it tolerable. It does not predict that
anyone else will pick it up, finish a run, find the output trustworthy, or pay.

**Summary**: across five load-bearing hypotheses, the count of real-evidence
items (specific past behavior with time/money/risk by a non-author operator) is
**zero**. Every hypothesis is currently propped up by either a compliment, a
hypothetical, a founder-authored category map, or self-grading.

## Bad-Data Signals Flagged

The case file is dense with bad data. Quoting verbatim:

1. **Compliment, hypothetical future.** > "a friend at a Series B AI startup said
   'I'd run this on our GTM next week.' Another friend at a YC-network startup
   said 'this is the kind of thing we'd pay for.'" Two friends, two
   future-conditional verbs ("I'd run," "we'd pay"), zero past behavior. Friends
   are the highest-bias prospect category; they are negatively predictive of
   purchase because they are filtering for the founder's feelings, not their own
   need. Fitzpatrick's first rule applies directly: this is people being polite
   about an idea, not people describing their lives.

2. **Vanity engagement.** > "LinkedIn posts about gtmstack draw modest engagement
   (low hundreds of impressions, single-digit reactions, occasional 'this is
   cool / I'd love to try this' replies)." Impressions are vanity. Single-digit
   reactions are vanity. "This is cool / I'd love to try this" is the canonical
   Mom Test compliment + hypothetical future — a polite affirmation followed by
   a future-conditional that has not converted to a single live-problem run.
   The input itself confirms zero conversion: "No replies have converted to
   running gtmstack on a live GTM problem."

3. **Build-as-evidence (founder-framing).** > "Project artifacts as of audit:
   5 books encoded ... ~30 case files across books, 11+ persisted run reports,
   3 multi-book synthesis deliverables ... v0→v1 strict-mode rubric upgrade
   complete ... cross-judge mode shipped, public-repo polish complete." This is
   the most subtle bad-data signal in the case. Counting build outputs in a
   demand-evidence audit is a category error: shipping more rubrics does not
   create more demand. The input correctly catches itself one sentence later:
   "These are build outputs, not demand outputs." Flag this anyway because
   founders re-fall into this trap; the existence of the artifacts will keep
   feeling like progress until the scoreboard explicitly excludes them.

4. **Self-grading.** > "The author has run gtmstack on two showcase cases ...
   Author's judgment: both syntheses 'feel right' and 'align with each company's
   observable strategic moves.' The author also wrote the framework encodings,
   the prompts, the rubrics, the case files, AND judges the outputs. No external
   operator has independently validated this. The author is grading their own
   homework." "Feels right" is the most-cited zero-evidence phrase in customer
   conversations. The case file already names the contamination — the author
   owns every layer of the stack — but the contaminated judgment is still
   functioning as the operative signal that the synthesis is good. It is not
   evidence; it is the founder's hope.

5. **Founder-authored category map.** > "gtmstack occupies a slot no competitor
   occupies — the only tool that runs canonical GTM frameworks as runnable
   rubrics with multi-book synthesis." A competitive map written by the founder
   describing why the founder's product is unique is, by Mom Test definition,
   founder-framing-as-evidence. Real positioning evidence comes from prospects
   describing the alternatives they considered, not from the founder mapping
   the field. The parenthetical — "AI-strategic-thinking peers (uncertain —
   possibly nothing exists yet)" — is honest, but the honesty does not rescue
   the underlying claim from being a hypothesis dressed as a finding.

## Past-Behavior Probes

For each hypothesis, the questions a non-friend operator should be asked about
specific events in their past. None of these are hypothetical.

**For H1 (Series B/C operators value 25-min vs $20K-$50K consulting):**
- "Walk me through the last time you faced a strategic GTM question — beachhead
  choice, positioning, segment prioritization. What did you actually do? Did
  you write a doc? Hire a consultant? Run a board offsite? What was the cost in
  hours, dollars, or political capital?"
- "When was the last time you paid (in money or in your own time) to get
  outside thinking on a GTM strategy question? Who did you pay? What did you
  get? Was it worth it?"
- "What did you reach for the last time you needed to decide between two
  strategic directions? If you used a framework or a book, which one, and what
  did you do with the output?"

**For H2 (gtmstack occupies an empty competitive slot):**
- "When the strategy question I just described comes up, what tools or sources
  do you currently use? Walk me through your last attempt — what did you open
  first, and where did you give up?"
- "What's the last thing you tried that promised to compress strategy work and
  didn't deliver? What specifically failed?"
- "Have you ever paid for, or seriously evaluated, an AI tool that claimed to
  do strategic thinking? What happened?"

**For H3 (multi-book synthesis is correct / useful for real operators):**
- "Take the last hard strategy question you faced. Run gtmstack on it now,
  before we talk about it. After you read the synthesis, walk me through which
  parts you would actually act on, which parts you would discard, and which
  parts told you something you did not already know." (This is the only probe
  that breaks the self-grading trap; it requires the operator to act on a
  problem the author did not pre-frame.)
- "Of the synthesis you just read, what's the prescription you most disagree
  with, and why?"

**For H4 (gstack audience overlaps with gtmstack audience):**
- "How did you find gstack? When did you install it? When was the last time
  you actually invoked it? On what problem?"
- "Who in your network would you forward gtmstack to? Walk me through who they
  are and what GTM problem they last described to you out loud."

**For H5 (dogfooding generalizes):**
- "When you read the showcase deliverables (cursor-2026, anthropic-brazil-2026),
  did the prescriptions match what you would have said, contradict what you
  would have said, or tell you something new? Be specific about which
  paragraph." (Probes for whether the synthesis is decoration vs. signal for
  someone who is not the author.)

## The Big Three

Three falsifying questions for the highest-priority prospects (non-friend
operators at Series B/C SaaS or AI-dev-tools companies). Each is paired with the
specific observable answer that would prove the underlying hypothesis WRONG.

**Q1 — targets H1 (operators will pay time or money for 25-minute synthesis).**
"Walk me through the last time a hard GTM strategy question came up at your
company. What did you do? How long did it take? What did it cost in hours,
dollars, or political capital?"
**Falsifying answer:** "It came up two months ago. I wrote a one-page memo in an
afternoon, sent it to the CEO, and we made the call in our next 1:1. We didn't
need outside help and we wouldn't have used it." If the operator's existing
process is one-afternoon-memo-with-the-CEO, the 25-minutes-vs-3-weeks comparison
is a comparison to a job that doesn't exist. The hypothesis assumes the
alternative is $20K consulting; the falsifying answer says the alternative is a
one-page Google Doc and a 1:1.

**Q2 — targets H3 (multi-book synthesis prescribes moves a real operator would
take, not just moves they would nod at).**
"Take a real strategic question you face right now. Run gtmstack on it before
this call. When we talk, walk me through the synthesis paragraph by paragraph
and tell me which prescriptions you'll act on this quarter, which you'll
discard, and which told you something you did not already believe."
**Falsifying answer:** "I read the synthesis. It's well-written. Most of it I
already knew or had already decided. There's nothing in here I'm going to do
this quarter that I wasn't already going to do." This answer is the silent
killer of a strategy-diagnostic tool: not "wrong," but "redundant." If a real
operator running it on a real problem produces no acted-on prescription, the
synthesis is decoration. Repeated across 5 of 10 operators, H3 is dead.

**Q3 — targets H1 commitment (the part Fitzpatrick cares most about).**
"Would you be willing to schedule a 30-minute working session next week where
you bring a real problem, we run gtmstack live, and either you commit a paid
pilot of $1K for me to handhold a synthesis on three more problems, or you
introduce me to two peers who face the same kind of question?"
**Falsifying answer:** "Sure, send me the link, I'll see if I have time."
Fitzpatrick: a politely-deferred meeting with no commitment of time, money, or
referral is Level 0 on the commitment ladder — it is the prospect being polite,
not the prospect buying. If the answer to Q3 is "send me a link" instead of
"yes, here's my calendar / here's $1K / here are two intros," then for that
operator demand has not been demonstrated, regardless of what they said about
the synthesis itself. This is the question whose answer the founder is most
likely to misread — "yes, send me a link" feels like progress. It is not.

## Commitment Ladder Position

**Current state: Level 0 — polite agreement to keep talking.** Across every
prospect interaction described in the inputs, no commitment of time, money, or
political capital has been made. Specifically:
- Friends ("I'd run this," "this is the kind of thing we'd pay for") committed
  no time and no calendar slot. Level 0.
- LinkedIn replies ("this is cool / I'd love to try this") committed no time
  past the reply itself. Level 0.
- The author's own dogfooding is not on this ladder; the author is the maker,
  not a prospect.

The case is not Level 1 ("their time on a longer follow-up call") because no
follow-up call has happened. It is not Level 2 ("a referral to a peer with the
problem worse") because no referral has occurred. It is not Level 3 (an intro to
the budget-holder) because no intro has happened. It is unambiguously Level 0.

**Next advancement: Level 2 or Level 4, skipping over Level 1.** The single most
diagnostic next move is to get **one non-friend operator to run gtmstack live on
a real problem they actually face** — not a problem the founder pre-framed —
and then either commit a paid pilot (Level 4) or refer two peers (Level 2). The
specific ask:

> "Pick a real strategic question you're facing this week. Block 45 minutes.
> I'll be on the call but not driving — you run gtmstack yourself. After we read
> the synthesis together, you tell me which prescriptions you will act on this
> quarter. If two or more, you commit either $1K for me to handhold three more
> syntheses for you (Level 4 paid pilot) or you intro me to two peers who face
> the same kind of question (Level 2 referral). If zero or one, we both walk
> away knowing the synthesis isn't ready for outside operators yet."

Notable: skipping Level 1 (a longer follow-up call) is deliberate. Level 1 is
the trap level — it feels like advancement and is not. Level 2 (referral) and
Level 4 (paid pilot) are the only signals worth chasing because both cost the
prospect something. The author's ~10-15 hrs/week part-time constraint makes Level
4 hard to fulfill at scale, but a single Level 4 conversion would do more for
the demand hypothesis than ten Level 1 follow-up calls.

## Bad-Mouth Test

What a smart, skeptical friend would warn the author about. Three observations,
including one structural skepticism about the case's own framing.

1. **External: "You are confusing build velocity with demand velocity."** A smart
   friend reads the artifact list — five books, thirty cases, eleven run
   reports, v0→v1 rubric upgrades, cross-judge mode, public-repo polish — and
   says: "You shipped a lot. You shipped to nobody. The fact that you can keep
   shipping does not mean anyone is asking you to. The next month of building is
   probably wasted unless you spend it on getting one operator to run this on a
   real problem." This is the most likely failure mode for a part-time
   open-source author with strong build instincts and no sales pipeline:
   producing more rubrics and calling it traction.

2. **External: "Your sample of enthusiastic listeners is selected for politeness,
   not for purchase intent."** Friends and LinkedIn followers are the two
   highest-politeness-bias prospect categories. A smart friend says: "Until a
   stranger who has nothing to gain from your good feelings runs gtmstack on a
   live problem and writes back with criticism, you have no signal. The people
   who like you most are the people whose feedback matters least."

3. **STRUCTURAL SKEPTICISM (v1 strict).** The case's own framing of the demand
   thesis contains a load-bearing structural assumption that the audit must
   surface. The case file says: > "Establish 'books-as-evals' as a category — the
   gtmstack repo as the canonical example, the pattern (framework.md /
   prompt.md / rubric.json / cases/) as the reusable shape." That sentence
   bakes in two structural assumptions stacked on top of each other:

   - **First assumption:** that "books-as-evals" is a category an operator would
     name, choose, and pay for. It is currently a structure the founder finds
     elegant. There is zero evidence in the inputs that any operator has ever
     described their need in those words, asked for that shape, or chosen it
     over alternatives. A category that the founder has named and that no
     prospect has named is not a category yet; it is a personal aesthetic.
   - **Second assumption (compounded):** that becoming "the canonical example"
     of that not-yet-validated category is itself the goal — i.e., the company's
     North Star is category-defining rather than user-serving. This is the Play
     Bigger trap: assuming the strategic move is to define a category before
     proving that anyone has the problem the category is supposed to solve. A
     smart friend says: "Drop the 'canonical example' frame entirely. Replace
     it with 'one paying operator running this on one live problem.' If you can't
     get the second, the first doesn't matter."

   The structural skepticism is therefore: **the company describes its
   opportunity in terms of a category it has named and that no prospect has
   named. Until a non-author operator describes their need in language that
   matches gtmstack's category language, the category itself is founder-framing,
   not market observation.** This is the language-trap the v1 rubric is
   specifically designed to surface.

## Confidence and Open Questions

**Confidence in quality of demand evidence in the inputs: 2 / 10.**
The inputs contain zero specific past-behavior signals from non-author operators.
The only Level-1+ commitment in the entire dataset is the author's own
dogfooding, which by Mom Test definition is not customer evidence. The case
file is unusually honest about this — the author flags self-grading, dogfooding
n=1, and the artifact-vs-demand distinction explicitly — but honest naming of
contamination does not remove the contamination.

**Confidence in testability of the load-bearing hypotheses: 7 / 10.**
The hypotheses are testable in principle. H1 and H3 in particular have
straightforward falsifying experiments: ten non-friend operators, ten live
problems, count how many produce acted-on prescriptions and how many convert to
Level 2+ commitments. The bottleneck is operator recruitment, not test design.
The 10-15 hrs/week constraint is real but not blocking — five operator
conversations spread across a month is well within that budget. The reason the
score is not higher is that the author has not yet run a single such
conversation, and Mom Test conversations are skill-dependent; the first three
will be partial-quality data even if the questions are right.

**Open questions (each names a specific fact that, if known, would change the
audit):**

1. **Of the ~200 LinkedIn impressions on the most recent gtmstack post, how
   many converted to a `git clone` of the repo, and of those clones, how many
   resulted in a successful `/gtm-office-hours` skill invocation that produced
   a synthesis document?** This question replaces the vanity-engagement signal
   with a specific past-behavior conversion ratio. If the answer is 0/0, then
   LinkedIn distribution is not even producing top-of-funnel; if it's 5
   clones / 1 completion, that is a specific install-to-completion ratio worth
   tracking. The case file admits the instrumentation gap is itself a signal —
   the open question is whether the author will instrument it or continue
   inferring from impressions.

2. **PRIOR-CONVERSATION-FLAW (v1 strict).** **What was the actual context of the
   two friend quotes ("I'd run this on our GTM next week"; "this is the kind of
   thing we'd pay for")?** Specifically: (a) was the prospect aware they were
   being interviewed for a demand-validation audit, or did they think they were
   giving a friend feedback on a side project? (b) was the founder in the room
   or otherwise framing the request, priming the prospect to evaluate fit-to-
   goal rather than independent need? (c) was the prospect aware that "I'd run
   this" had a future cost — their time, their reputation if they introduced
   it to a peer and it flopped? **If the answer to any of (a)/(b)/(c)
   implicates founder-framing, the quote is bad data regardless of its
   content** — a friend told the author what they knew the author wanted to
   hear, and the future-conditional "I'd" verb is the most plausible polite
   response from someone who does not want to embarrass a friend over a side
   project. Until those contexts are reconstructed and the quotes re-evaluated,
   both of the case's headline "validation" quotes are provisionally bad data
   and should be excluded from the demand calculus entirely.

3. **What is the median time-to-completion for a non-author operator running
   `/gtm-office-hours` end-to-end, and what is the dropout point?** The skill
   has not been measured end-to-end with a single external user. Three
   plausible failure modes: (a) the 10-minute interview front-end feels too
   long and operators bail before the books run; (b) the synthesis output is
   too long to read; (c) the synthesis output reads as well-written but does
   not produce an acted-on prescription. Knowing which of these is the dropout
   point would change the audit materially — if (a), the GTM problem is
   activation; if (c), the GTM problem is whether the product works at all.
   The author cannot answer this from inside; one external operator instrumented
   end-to-end resolves it.

---

**Summary verdict.** The case as presented contains zero items of real demand
evidence and a half-dozen items of bad data that the case file itself, in its
more honest moments, names as bad data. The load-bearing hypothesis is H3 — that
the multi-book synthesis prescribes moves a real operator would act on — and it
is currently propped up entirely by the author grading their own homework. The
single highest-leverage next move is one paying-or-time-committing non-friend
operator running gtmstack on one live problem they faced, evaluated by whether
the operator acts on at least two prescriptions or refers two peers within four
weeks. Everything else — more rubrics, more case files, more LinkedIn posts,
v2 strict-mode upgrades, hosted-version monetization design — is a build-output
distraction until that one conversion happens.
